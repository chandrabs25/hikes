You will cover a total of around 48.5 km in five trekking days, during which you will gain approximately 6,000 ft (5,600 ft to 11,600 ft). Every day will include ascents, descents and boulder sections. Some difficult sections on this trek require you to be alert and trek carefully.
Terrain:
The Brammah Valley Trek involves long days of trekking. Almost every day, you trek 8 to 10 km. Sometimes even 12 to 13 km.
The first day has good ascents and descents. The last two days are also long, and all downhill. This can be hard on your knees.
There are multiple stream crossings on the trek with no bridges. Being nimble on your feet is extremely important while crossing these streams.
There are some narrow ledge sections. Not too technical but our team will be there to guide you safely.
Sometimes, from Kiddar Maidan, we take a steep climb up to a ridge. This gives you a magical view. It’s not compulsory. But if you're fit, go for it. Kiddar Maidan itself is very beautiful, so don’t worry if you skip the climb.
Please note: If you are trekking with Indiahikes, our Trek Leaders and Technical Guides will provide you with all necessary guidance, support, and technical gear such as micro spikes, gaiters, helmets, ropes, etc. wherever required.
Weather:
Rainfall or snowfall lasting hours can change the entire trek dynamic. Rain can flood the trails or make them too slushy to walk safely on, while snowfall can obstruct previously made paths, requiring fresh tracks to be cut on the snow.
Too much rain can flood the entire Kedar Maidan campsite, since it sits on a river delta. That’s why we avoid running this trek during the monsoon — unlike most Kashmir treks, where monsoon is actually the best time to go..
Please note: The safety of our trekkers is paramount. Our technical team, in conjunction with the Trek Leader, constantly monitors the weather conditions and makes informed decisions about proceeding with or aborting the trek. If the weather deteriorates, the team may need to turn back from any campsite to ensure the safety of all trekkers.
Altitude:
At the summit, Brammah Valley reaches 11,600 ft. That is not too high. You start at around 6,000 feet. You gain about 2,000 feet each day. This slow gain helps your body adjust. So, altitude sickness is less likely.
However, at altitudes above 10,000 ft, the chances of being hit by Acute Mountain Sickness are real. Altitude sickness can happen to anybody, first-time trekkers as well as experienced trekkers. Fitness helps in making your trek easier. But do not forget that even a fit person runs an equal risk of developing AMS.
Here’s a Complete guide to Acute Mountains Sickness, HAPE and HACE
Please Note: The Indiahikes team is equipped with high-altitude medical kits, oxygen cylinders, and stretchers to handle any altitude-related emergencies.
Emergency Exits:
On the Brammah Valley Trek, the only exit point is Sounder. So, if there are emergencies on the trail, you need to return all the way to the trek start point.
Closest Hospital to the Brammah Valley Trek:
In case of a medical emergency, the closest hospital is at Kishtwar. It’s about 2 km from the trek start point, Sounder. If you need serious medical attention, head to Srinagar for better facilities. Srinagar is a 7 hr drive from Sounder.
The Indiahikes team will ensure your safety throughout the journey. However, we believe that safety is a shared responsibility. We need your active participation and attention to safety protocols to ensure a safe and successful trek.
Please inform your Trek Leader about any pre-existing medical conditions or health concerns, no matter how minor they may seem. This will enable us to take necessary precautions and ensure your safety throughout the trek.

Check both before you submit. It'll save you the back and forth.
4. What happens if your fitness records get rejected?
You can still trek. But your Trek Leader and Slope Manager will be informed about your fitness levels in advance. If you fall behind the group by 45 minutes and are visibly struggling, your Trek Leader may send you back. That's a call none of us want to make. Children below 14 years are not required to submit fitness records. However, parents must ensure that children remain physically active and prepared for the trek.

3. Clothes:
Wearing layers is the mantra in the mountains. Layers give you maximum protection from all elements. And when the weather changes in the mountains (as it happens every few hours), you take off or put on layers as required.
Base Layer:
3 T-Shirts:
Wear one T-shirt and carry two. Carry full-sleeve dry-fit T-shirts (preferably collared). These prevent your arms and neck from getting sunburnt. In the rarified air on the trek, especially at high altitudes, UV rays can burn you in no time.
Dry-fit T-shirts quickly dry your sweat, they are easy to wash and in case of a rainy day, they dry quicker. Round neck T-shirts are ok, but collared ones are better.
| Cotton or Synthetic? As Indians, we love cotton. Down in the plains when the heat is blistering 40°C it makes sense to wear cotton. But it takes a long time to dry when it gets wet. In the mountains, where it is cooler, synthetic is what you wear. They wick sweat rapidly and keep you dry. (But they do tend to smell quickly, so carry a roll-on deodorant with you.)
| Pro Tip: If you are extra susceptible to cold, you could get a set of thermal inners. In our experience, wearing two T-shirts over another works as a better thermal. And they save you weight and space since you’re already carrying them.

You will need 2 light fleece layers and 1 full-sleeve light sweater. Do not get your grandma-stitched sweaters, which can be very heavy. You need sweaters and fleece jackets that can fold into compact rolls.
1 Outer Layer:
A padded jacket serves the purpose here. You don’t need a water-resistant material. But you need an outer padded jacket that keeps the wind and cold out. Ensure your padded jacket has a hood as well.

| Buying Tip: Go for pants with zippered pockets. They come in handy to keep your phone, handkerchief, or pocket snacks.

1. Sunglasses:
Sunglasses are to prevent snow blindness. On a trek like Brammah Valley, Expect to walk on stretches of snow, especially during July. A small overexposure to direct sunlight on snow can lead to snow blindness (about a half hour’s exposure). That’s because fallen snow is like thousands of mirrors that reflect direct UV rays. So you need sunglasses with UV protection.
| Tip: Try getting sunglasses that wrap around instead of those that have openings on the side. Even peripheral UV ray exposure is not a good idea.
Wear sunglasses if the trekking day is bright and sunny (on open sections, meadows). On a snowy section, you must never take off your sunglasses until the section has been fully crossed.

2. Suncap:
A sun cap is mandatory. Trekking without a sun cap can lead to headaches, sunstrokes, quick dehydration and a sharp drop in trekking performance.
| Tip: In the mountains, the general rule is to keep your head covered at all times. During the day a sun cap protects you from the harsh rays of the sun (it is doubly stronger with naked UV rays). A sun cap keeps your body temperature in balance. In the evening/early morning, the reverse is true. Your head loses your body heat quickly. A woollen cap prevents heat from dissipating from your head.

On a trek like Gure Valley Trek, you are going to be handling snow quite a bit during July. You’ll need gloves to grip something or to steady yourself in snow. Get synthetic hand gloves that have waterproofing on the outside and a padded lining on the inside. Your ears are sensitive too, so a woollen head cap that covers your ears is essential. A balaclava is a modern version of the woollen cap. It covers your ears, neck and parts of your face as well. Do not get a woollen cap that only covers your head.
